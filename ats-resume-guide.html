Why Your Resume Gets Rejected by ATS Software (And How to Fix It in 20 Minutes)

6 min read · Resume Tips

Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) don't reject resumes for being "bad" in a human sense. They reject — or more accurately, deprioritize — resumes that don't match the structure and language the software is looking for. Understanding the mechanics removes most of the mystery.

What an ATS actually does

Most ATS platforms parse your resume into fields: contact info, work history, education, skills. They then compare the text against the job description, often scoring or ranking candidates by keyword overlap before a human ever opens the file. If your resume's formatting confuses the parser, or your wording doesn't overlap with the job posting, you can be a strong candidate and still rank poorly.

The 20-minute checklist

1. Match the job description's exact terms (5 min)

If the posting says "stakeholder management" and your resume says "client coordination," a keyword-matching system may not connect the two. Read the posting and mirror its specific phrasing where it's accurate to your experience — this is the single highest-impact change you can make.

2. Use standard section headers (2 min)

"Professional Experience," "Education," "Skills" — stick to conventional headers. Creative alternatives like "My Journey" or "What I Bring" can confuse parsers into misfiling your content.

3. Avoid tables, columns, and graphics for core content (5 min)

Multi-column layouts and text boxes often parse out of order or get dropped entirely. A single-column layout with clear headers is the safest format for ATS, even if it looks plainer.

4. Spell out acronyms at least once (3 min)

If you write "SQL," also consider whether the posting uses "Structured Query Language" anywhere — unlikely, but for less common acronyms in your field, including both versions once covers your bases.

5. Save as the requested format (1 min)

If the application allows both, .docx generally parses more reliably than PDF across different ATS platforms, though most modern systems handle both. If a posting specifies a format, follow it exactly.

6. Use the scanner to check your overlap (4 min)

Paste the job description and your resume text into a keyword match tool to see, concretely, which terms from the posting appear in your resume and which don't. This isn't about gaming the system — it's about confirming that the language you already use to describe your experience actually overlaps with how the role is described.

The goal isn't to stuff keywords — it's to make sure the real overlap between your experience and the role is visible to both the software and the person reading it next.

What not to do

  • Don't add skills or keywords you can't speak to in an interview — overlap should reflect real experience
  • Don't use white text or hidden keywords — most modern ATS platforms detect this, and it can disqualify you outright
  • Don't assume one resume version works for every application — a 10-minute tailoring pass per application is worth it for roles you actually want

None of this replaces having relevant experience — but it does ensure that the experience you have is actually visible to the systems and people deciding whether to move you forward.
