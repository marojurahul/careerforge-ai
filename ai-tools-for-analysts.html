5 Ways to Use Claude or ChatGPT in Daily Business Analyst Work (With Real Prompts)

7 min read · AI Tools

Most guides to "using AI at work" stay vague — "ask it to summarize things," "use it for brainstorming." Here are five specific, repeatable uses pulled from actual operations and analyst workflows, with prompts you can adapt directly.

1. Turning raw logs into a structured summary

If you handle escalations, support tickets, or incident logs, you've likely spent hours manually grouping similar issues. An AI assistant can do a first pass in seconds.

"Here are 30 escalation log entries [paste]. Group them by root cause, list the most frequent categories, and flag any that appear more than 3 times this week compared to last week."

You still review and validate the output — that's the analyst's job — but the categorization step that used to take an hour now takes ten minutes.

2. Drafting variance report narratives

Numbers are easy to pull; writing the "why" narrative for a budget-vs-actual report is the slow part. Give the AI the numbers and ask it to draft the explanation.

"Here's this month's budget vs actual by category [paste table]. Draft a short narrative explaining the three largest variances in plain language for a management audience, noting where further investigation may be needed."

3. Writing SQL or Power BI DAX from a plain-language request

Even if you know SQL well, having an AI draft a first version of a complex query — especially with multiple joins or window functions — saves time and catches edge cases you might not think of immediately.

"Write a SQL query that finds customers who placed an order in the last 30 days but had no orders in the prior 90 days. Use CTEs for readability and add comments explaining each step."

4. Documenting a process for the first time

If you've ever been asked to "write up the process" for something you do automatically, you know how hard it is to externalize. Describe the process out loud (or in rough notes) and have the AI structure it.

"I'll describe a process in rough order — turn this into a numbered, step-by-step SOP document with clear ownership at each step, and flag any steps where the order seems unclear or risky."

5. Practicing how you talk about your work

This one is less about output and more about preparation. Before interviews, use an AI assistant to pressure-test how you describe your projects.

"I'm going to describe a project I built. Ask me three follow-up questions an interviewer might ask, focused on impact and my specific role — then tell me which of my answers were vague."

How to talk about this in interviews

Don't oversell it as "I use AI for everything." Instead, be specific: name the tool, the task, and the measurable result. "I used Claude to draft a first-pass categorization of escalation logs, which cut my weekly reporting time by roughly 40%" is concrete, credible, and exactly the kind of evidence an AI-focused team wants to hear.

The goal isn't to replace your judgment with AI output — it's to spend less time on the mechanical first draft and more time on the analysis that actually requires you.
